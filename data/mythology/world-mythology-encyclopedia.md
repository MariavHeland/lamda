## 1. GREEK MYTHOLOGY

### Cosmogony and Primordial Deities

Greek cosmogony begins with **Chaos**, the yawning void. From Chaos emerged the first primordial beings:

- **Gaia** (Earth) — the foundation of all; mother of the Titans, Giants, and Pontus
- **Tartarus** — the deep abyss beneath the underworld, both a place and a deity
- **Eros** — primordial force of desire and procreation (distinct from the later Olympian Eros/Cupid)
- **Erebus** — primordial darkness
- **Nyx** (Night) — immensely powerful; even Zeus feared her. Mother of Thanatos (Death), Hypnos (Sleep), the Moirai (Fates), Nemesis, Eris (Strife), the Hesperides, and others
- **Uranus** (Sky) — son and consort of Gaia; father of the Titans
- **Pontus** (Sea) — primordial sea god, born from Gaia alone

### The Titans (Second Generation)

The twelve Titans, children of Uranus and Gaia:

| Titan | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Cronus** | Time, harvest, king of Titans | Castrated Uranus with an adamantine sickle; swallowed his children; overthrown by Zeus |
| **Rhea** | Motherhood, fertility | Consort of Cronus; hid infant Zeus in Crete |
| **Oceanus** | World-encircling river | Did not join the Titanomachy against Zeus |
| **Tethys** | Fresh water, nursing | Consort of Oceanus; mother of the river gods and Oceanids |
| **Hyperion** | Light | Father of Helios (Sun), Selene (Moon), Eos (Dawn) |
| **Theia** | Sight, shining | Consort of Hyperion |
| **Coeus** | Intellect, the axis of heaven | Father of Leto |
| **Phoebe** | Prophecy, radiance | Grandmother of Apollo and Artemis; associated with Delphi before Apollo |
| **Mnemosyne** | Memory | Mother of the nine Muses by Zeus |
| **Themis** | Divine law, order | Mother of the Horae (Seasons) and Moirai (Fates) by Zeus |
| **Crius** | Constellations | Father of Astraeus, Pallas, Perses |
| **Iapetus** | Mortality, craftsmanship | Father of Prometheus, Epimetheus, Atlas, Menoetius |

**Other notable Titans and Titan-offspring:**
- **Prometheus** — stole fire from the gods for humanity; punished by Zeus (eagle eating his liver daily on Mount Caucasus); freed by Heracles. A culture hero and symbol of defiance.
- **Epimetheus** — "afterthought"; accepted Pandora from Zeus
- **Atlas** — condemned to hold the sky on his shoulders; tricked by Heracles during the golden apples labor
- **Helios** — Titan of the sun who drove a golden chariot across the sky daily; his son Phaethon lost control of the chariot and was struck down by Zeus

**Dionysus** (Bacchus) — god of wine, ecstasy, theater, madness, and ritual frenzy. Son of Zeus and the mortal Semele. Often considered the thirteenth or replacement Olympian. His cult was ecstatic and transformative. Associated with grapes, ivy, thyrsus (fennel staff topped with a pinecone), leopard, and theatrical masks. His followers included the Maenads (frenzied women) and Satyrs.

**Hades** (Pluto) — lord of the underworld and the dead. Brother of Zeus and Poseidon. Not an Olympian (he resided in the underworld). Symbols: Helm of Darkness (invisibility), Cerberus, cypress, bident. Consort: Persephone (abducted her).

### Major Myths and Story Cycles

**The Titanomachy:** Ten-year war between the Olympian gods (led by Zeus) and the Titans (led by Cronus). Zeus freed the Cyclopes (who gave him the thunderbolt, Poseidon the trident, and Hades the Helm of Darkness) and the Hecatonchires (hundred-handed giants). The Titans were defeated and imprisoned in Tartarus.

**The Gigantomachy:** Battle between the Olympians and the Giants (Gigantes), born from Gaia's blood when Uranus was castrated. The gods needed a mortal's help to win; Heracles was essential. Key giants: Alcyoneus, Porphyrion, Enceladus (buried under Mount Etna).

**Prometheus and Pandora:** Prometheus shaped humans from clay, stole fire from Olympus. Zeus punished him and created Pandora, the first woman, given a jar (mistranslated as "box") containing all evils. She opened it, releasing suffering into the world; only Hope (Elpis) remained inside.

**The Trojan War:**
- **Cause:** The Judgment of Paris — Paris of Troy chose Aphrodite as the fairest goddess (over Hera and Athena), and was rewarded with Helen of Sparta, the most beautiful woman.
- **Key Greeks (Achaeans):** Agamemnon (commander), Menelaus (Helen's husband), Achilles (greatest warrior; invulnerable except his heel), Odysseus (cunning strategist), Ajax the Great, Diomedes, Patroclus (Achilles' beloved companion), Nestor.
- **Key Trojans:** Priam (king), Hector (greatest Trojan warrior), Paris, Aeneas (survived to found Rome in Roman tradition), Cassandra (prophetess cursed never to be believed).
- **Major Events:** Sacrifice of Iphigenia, Wrath of Achilles (withdrew from battle after Agamemnon took his war-prize Briseis), Death of Patroclus, Achilles kills Hector, Trojan Horse stratagem (Odysseus's idea), Fall of Troy.
- **Sources:** Homer's *Iliad* (covers a few weeks in the tenth year), the Epic Cycle (now mostly lost).

**The Odyssey:**
- Odysseus's ten-year journey home after Troy.
- Key episodes: Lotus Eaters, Cyclops Polyphemus (blinding him angered Poseidon), Aeolus and the bag of winds, Circe (sorceress who turned men to pigs), journey to the Underworld (Nekyia — meets Tiresias, Achilles, his mother Anticlea), Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, Cattle of Helios, Calypso's island (seven years), Phaeacians, return to Ithaca, slaughter of the suitors, reunion with Penelope.

**The Argonauts:**
- Jason's quest for the Golden Fleece in Colchis, aboard the ship Argo.
- Crew included Heracles, Orpheus, Castor and Pollux, Atalanta (in some versions), and many other heroes.
- Key episodes: Harpies and Phineus, Clashing Rocks (Symplegades), fire-breathing bulls, dragon's teeth warriors.
- Medea (Colchian princess, sorceress) helped Jason; later he betrayed her, leading to her horrific revenge (killing their children).

**The Labors of Heracles (Hercules):**
Assigned by King Eurystheus as penance for killing his family in a madness sent by Hera. Originally ten labors, expanded to twelve when Eurystheus disqualified two:

1. Slay the **Nemean Lion** (impervious hide; strangled it; wore its skin)
2. Slay the **Lernaean Hydra** (multi-headed serpent; grew two heads for each cut; cauterized stumps with help of Iolaus)
3. Capture the **Ceryneian Hind** (sacred to Artemis; golden antlers, bronze hooves)
4. Capture the **Erymanthian Boar** (drove it into deep snow)
5. Clean the **Augean Stables** in one day (diverted two rivers)
6. Slay the **Stymphalian Birds** (man-eating; bronze beaks; used Athena's rattle to startle them)
7. Capture the **Cretan Bull** (father of the Minotaur)
8. Steal the **Mares of Diomedes** (man-eating horses)
9. Obtain the **Belt of Hippolyta** (Amazon queen)
10. Obtain the **Cattle of Geryon** (three-bodied giant in the far west)
11. Steal the **Apples of the Hesperides** (golden apples; held the sky for Atlas)
12. Capture **Cerberus** from the Underworld (wrestled the three-headed dog)

**Perseus:**
- Son of Zeus and Danae (Zeus came as a shower of gold).
- Sent to kill Medusa (the mortal Gorgon whose gaze turned people to stone).
- Aided by Athena (polished shield as mirror), Hermes (winged sandals, kibisis bag), and the Nymphs.
- Beheaded Medusa; Pegasus and Chrysaor sprang from her neck.
- Rescued Andromeda from a sea monster (Cetus) sent by Poseidon.
- Accidentally killed his grandfather Acrisius with a discus (fulfilling prophecy).

**Theseus:**
- Son of Aegeus (or Poseidon) and Aethra; Athenian national hero.
- Slew the Minotaur in the Labyrinth of Crete with Ariadne's thread.
- Various adventures on road to Athens (Procrustes, Sciron, etc.).
- Abandoned Ariadne on Naxos; forgot to change sails from black to white, causing his father Aegeus to leap to his death (origin of the Aegean Sea's name).
- Later adventures with the Amazons, the Calydonian boar hunt, and the ill-fated attempt to abduct Persephone from the Underworld.

**Orpheus and Eurydice:** Orpheus descended to the Underworld to retrieve his dead wife Eurydice. His music charmed Hades and Persephone, who agreed to release her on the condition he not look back. He looked back at the threshold and lost her forever.

**Oedipus:** Fulfilled the prophecy of killing his father (Laius) and marrying his mother (Jocasta). Solved the Sphinx's riddle. Foundation of Sophocles' tragedies. His sons' conflict led to the Seven Against Thebes.

**The Myth of Persephone:** Hades abducted Persephone; Demeter searched for her, causing famine. Zeus brokered a deal: Persephone spends part of the year in the Underworld (winter) and part above (spring/summer). Aetiological myth explaining the seasons.

### The Underworld (Hades)

**Geography:**
- **River Styx** — river of hatred; oaths sworn on it were unbreakable; Achilles was dipped in it
- **River Acheron** — river of woe; Charon ferried the dead across it (fee: one obol coin placed in the mouth of the dead)
- **River Lethe** — river of forgetfulness; souls drank to forget their past lives before reincarnation
- **River Phlegethon** — river of fire
- **River Cocytus** — river of lamentation
- **Asphodel Meadows** — destination of ordinary, unremarkable souls

- **Isles of the Blessed** — for those who achieved Elysium three times through reincarnation

- **Ixion** — bound to a burning wheel for eternity

**Judges of the Dead:** Minos, Rhadamanthys, and Aeacus.

## 2. NORSE MYTHOLOGY

### Sources
Primary sources: the **Poetic Edda** (collection of Old Norse poems, 13th century Codex Regius) and the **Prose Edda** (written by Snorri Sturluson c. 1220). Also sagas, skaldic poetry, and archaeological evidence.

### Cosmology — Yggdrasil and the Nine Realms

**Yggdrasil** — the World Tree, an immense ash tree at the center of the cosmos. Three roots connect to three wells:
1. **Urdarbrunnr** (Well of Fate) — where the Norns dwell
2. **Mimisbrunnr** (Mimir's Well) — source of wisdom; Odin sacrificed an eye to drink from it
3. A root reaching into **Niflheim** near the spring Hvergelmir, gnawed by the dragon Nidhogg

**The Nine Realms:**

| Realm | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Asgard** | Home of the Aesir gods; contains Valhalla, Odin's hall |
| **Midgard** | World of humans; connected to Asgard by the Bifrost (rainbow bridge) |
| **Jotunheim** | Land of the giants (Jotnar) |
| **Vanaheim** | Home of the Vanir gods |
| **Alfheim** | Home of the light elves (Ljosalfar) |
| **Svartalfheim / Nidavellir** | Home of the dwarves (and dark elves in some sources); master craftsmen |
| **Niflheim** | Primordial realm of ice, mist, and cold |
| **Muspelheim** | Primordial realm of fire; home of the fire giant Surtr |
| **Helheim (Hel)** | Realm of the dead who did not die in battle; ruled by Hel |

### Creation Myth

In the beginning there was **Ginnungagap**, the yawning void between Niflheim (ice) and Muspelheim (fire). When ice and fire met, the frost giant **Ymir** was formed from the melting ice, along with the primordial cow **Audhumla**, who nourished Ymir with her milk. Audhumla licked the salty ice and uncovered **Buri**, grandfather of the gods. Buri's grandson was **Odin**, along with his brothers **Vili** and **Ve**. The three brothers slew Ymir and fashioned the world from his body:
- His flesh became the earth
- His blood became the seas
- His bones became the mountains
- His skull became the sky (held up by four dwarves)
- His brains became the clouds

They created the first humans, **Ask** (ash tree) and **Embla** (elm tree), giving them life, consciousness, and warmth.

### Loki

**Loki** is a complex, liminal figure — a Jotun (giant) who lives among the Aesir. He is the **trickster** of Norse mythology: cunning, shapeshifting, sometimes helpful and sometimes catastrophically destructive.

- Blood-brother of Odin
- Father (or in one case mother) of monstrous offspring: **Fenrir** (the great wolf), **Jormungandr** (the World Serpent), **Hel** (ruler of the dead), and **Sleipnir** (Odin's eight-legged horse, whom Loki bore as a mare)
- Caused Baldur's death by guiding the mistletoe dart
- Punished by being bound with the entrails of his own son, with a serpent dripping venom on his face (his wife Sigyn catches the venom in a bowl; when she empties it, the drips cause him to writhe, which causes earthquakes)
- Will break free at Ragnarok and lead the forces of chaos

### Ragnarok (Twilight of the Gods)

The prophesied end of the world, described primarily in the poem *Voluspa*:

**Precursors:** Fimbulwinter (three successive winters with no summers), moral collapse among humans, Baldur's death, Loki and Fenrir break free.

**The Battle:**
- Heimdall sounds Gjallarhorn
- The World Serpent rises from the sea, causing floods
- The ship Naglfar (made from the nails of the dead) carries the forces of chaos
- Surtr leads the fire giants across the Bifrost, destroying it
- **Odin vs. Fenrir** — Fenrir swallows Odin; Vidar avenges him
- **Thor vs. Jormungandr** — Thor kills the serpent but dies from its venom after nine steps
- **Freyr vs. Surtr** — Freyr falls (lacks his sword)
- **Heimdall vs. Loki** — they kill each other
- **Tyr vs. Garm** (the hound of Hel) — they kill each other
- Surtr sets the world ablaze

**Rebirth:** The world sinks into the sea but rises again, green and fertile. Baldur and Hodr return from the dead. Vidar and Vali survive. A new human pair, Lif and Lifthrasir, repopulate the world. A cycle of renewal.

### Heroes

**Sigurd (Siegfried):** Greatest Norse/Germanic hero. Raised by the smith Regin. Slew the dragon **Fafnir** (originally a dwarf transformed by greed for cursed gold). Tasted Fafnir's blood and could understand birds. Won the Valkyrie **Brynhild** but through a magic-induced mix-up married Gudrun. Betrayed and killed. His story is told in the *Volsunga Saga* and influenced Wagner's *Ring Cycle*.

**Beowulf:** Anglo-Saxon hero (related tradition). Slew the monster Grendel, Grendel's mother, and died fighting a dragon.

## 3. EGYPTIAN MYTHOLOGY

### Cosmology and Creation Myths

Egypt had multiple creation myths from different cult centers:

**Heliopolitan cosmogony (Ennead of Heliopolis):**
- **Nun** — primordial waters of chaos
- **Atum** (or Atum-Ra) emerged from Nun, standing on the primordial mound (Benben)
- Atum created **Shu** (air/dryness) and **Tefnut** (moisture) through sneezing/spitting
- Shu and Tefnut begat **Geb** (earth) and **Nut** (sky)
- Shu separated Geb and Nut (earth and sky)
- Geb and Nut bore **Osiris**, **Isis**, **Set**, and **Nephthys** — the Ennead (nine gods)

**Hermopolitan cosmogony (Ogdoad):** Eight primordial deities in pairs — Nun/Naunet (water), Huh/Hauhet (infinity), Kuk/Kauket (darkness), Amun/Amunet (hiddenness). Their interactions created the mound from which the sun emerged.

**Memphite cosmogony:** **Ptah** created the world through thought (in his heart) and speech (the word), an early concept of creation through logos.

**Theban cosmogony:** **Amun** as the supreme hidden creator, later merged with Ra as **Amun-Ra**.

| **Amun** | Hidden power, wind, king of the gods | Ram or man with double-plumed crown | Rose to supreme importance in New Kingdom as Amun-Ra; "King of the Gods" |
| **Nut** | Sky | Woman arched over the earth, covered in stars | Swallowed the sun each evening; gave birth to it each morning |
| **Geb** | Earth | Man lying beneath Nut, often depicted with green skin | Father of Osiris, Isis, Set, Nephthys |

### The Osiris Myth

The central myth of Egyptian religion:
1. Osiris was a wise and just king of Egypt, beloved by all.
2. His brother **Set**, jealous, created a beautiful chest and tricked Osiris into lying in it during a banquet. Set sealed it and threw it into the Nile.
3. Isis searched everywhere and found the chest embedded in a tamarisk tree in Byblos (Phoenicia).
4. Set discovered the body, dismembered it into 14 (or 42) pieces, and scattered them across Egypt.
5. Isis and Nephthys found all the pieces (except the phallus, eaten by a fish) and reassembled Osiris. Isis fashioned a replacement phallus and magically conceived **Horus**.
6. Osiris could not return to the living world but became lord and judge of the dead in the **Duat** (underworld).
7. Horus grew up in hiding, then challenged Set for the throne. After many contests (including Set's loss of his testicles and Horus's loss of an eye), the gods judged in Horus's favor.

### Afterlife — The Journey Through the Duat

Egyptian afterlife beliefs were complex and evolved over millennia:

**Mummification:** Preserving the body was essential for the ka (life force) and ba (personality/soul) to survive. Anubis presided. Organs were stored in canopic jars (four sons of Horus guarded them).

**The Book of the Dead (Book of Coming Forth by Day):** Collection of spells, prayers, and instructions placed with the deceased to navigate the afterlife. Key chapters include:
- Spells for passing through gates guarded by demons
- Chapter 125: **The Weighing of the Heart** — the most critical moment
  - The deceased's heart was placed on one side of a scale, the feather of Ma'at on the other
  - **Thoth** recorded the result
  - If the heart was lighter than or equal to the feather (a just life), the soul was admitted to the **Field of Reeds** (Aaru) — an idealized version of Egypt
  - If the heart was heavier (burdened by sin), it was devoured by **Ammit** ("Devourer of the Dead," a chimera of crocodile, lion, and hippopotamus), and the soul ceased to exist
  - The **Negative Confession**: the deceased declared 42 sins they had not committed before 42 judges

**Apophis (Apep):** The great serpent of chaos who attacked Ra's solar barque each night as it traveled through the underworld. The daily sunrise meant Ra had triumphed again. Set sometimes helped defend against Apophis.

### Sacred Animals and Symbols

- **Scarab beetle (Khepri)** — symbol of transformation and the rising sun; Khepri rolled the sun across the sky
- **Eye of Horus (Wadjet)** — protection, healing, royal power; each piece represented a fraction (1/2, 1/4, etc.)
- **Eye of Ra** — destructive aspect of the sun; associated with Sekhmet, Hathor, Bastet
- **Ankh** — symbol of life; held by gods
- **Djed pillar** — stability; backbone of Osiris
- **Was scepter** — power and dominion
- **Uraeus** — rearing cobra on pharaoh's crown; protective symbol
- **Cats** (Bastet), **ibises** (Thoth), **falcons** (Horus), **jackals** (Anubis), **crocodiles** (Sobek), **cows** (Hathor), **rams** (Amun/Khnum), **vultures** (Nekhbet) — all held sacred

## 4. HINDU MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

The **Vedas** (Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, Atharvaveda) — oldest sacred texts, c. 1500-1200 BCE. The **Upanishads** (philosophical texts). The great epics: **Mahabharata** (which contains the **Bhagavad Gita**) and **Ramayana**. The **Puranas** (mythological encyclopedias). The **Agamas** and **Tantras**.

### The Trimurti

The three supreme aspects of the divine:

**Brahma** (The Creator):
- Four-headed, four-armed; carries the Vedas, a water pot (kamandalu), prayer beads (mala), and a lotus
- Consort: **Saraswati** (knowledge, arts, music)
- Vehicle (vahana): **Hamsa** (swan/goose)
- Rarely worshipped directly today; only one major temple (Pushkar, Rajasthan)
- Created the universe at the beginning of each cosmic cycle

**Vishnu** (The Preserver):
- Blue-skinned; four-armed; holds **Sudarshana Chakra** (discus), **Shankha** (conch), **Gada** (mace), and **Padma** (lotus)
- Consort: **Lakshmi** (wealth, fortune, prosperity)
- Vehicle: **Garuda** (divine eagle)
- Reclines on **Shesha** (cosmic serpent) on the cosmic ocean
- Descends as **avatars** when dharma is threatened

**The Dashavatara (Ten Avatars of Vishnu):**

| Avatar | Form | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| **Matsya** | Fish | Saved Manu (first man) and the Vedas from a great flood |
| **Kurma** | Tortoise | Supported Mount Mandara during the Churning of the Ocean of Milk (Samudra Manthan) |
| **Varaha** | Boar | Rescued the Earth (Bhudevi) from the demon Hiranyaksha who had dragged it to the cosmic ocean floor |
| **Narasimha** | Man-lion | Slew the demon Hiranyakashipu (who had a boon making him immune to death by man or beast, indoors or outdoors, by day or by night — Narasimha killed him at twilight, on a threshold, on his lap) |
| **Vamana** | Dwarf Brahmin | Tricked the demon king Bali by asking for three steps of land, then grew to cosmic size and covered all three worlds |
| **Parashurama** | Warrior with axe | Destroyed the corrupt Kshatriya warrior class 21 times |
| **Rama** | Prince/King | Hero of the Ramayana; ideal man and king; defeated the demon king Ravana |
| **Krishna** | Cowherd, prince, charioteer | Central figure of the Mahabharata; delivered the Bhagavad Gita; playful child who stole butter; romantic youth with the gopis; wise king |
| **Buddha** | Teacher (in some lists) | Various interpretations; some lists substitute Balarama |
| **Kalki** | Future avatar on white horse | Will appear at the end of the Kali Yuga to destroy evil and restart the cycle |

**Shiva** (The Destroyer/Transformer):
- Complex deity with many forms: the ascetic meditator, the cosmic dancer (Nataraja), the householder with Parvati, the fierce Bhairava
- **Symbols:** Third eye (destroys with its opening), trident (trishula), crescent moon in matted hair, the Ganges flowing from his hair, serpents, tiger skin, drum (damaru), blue throat (Neelakantha — from drinking the poison Halahala during the churning of the ocean to save the world)
- Consort: **Parvati** (also manifests as **Durga**, **Kali**, **Uma**, **Gauri**)
- Vehicle: **Nandi** (bull)
- Sons: **Ganesha** and **Kartikeya** (Murugan/Skanda)
- Performs the **Tandava** (cosmic dance of creation and destruction)
- Worshipped as the **Lingam** (abstract representation of cosmic creative energy)

### Other Major Deities

**Ganesha:**
- Elephant-headed god of beginnings, wisdom, learning, obstacle removal
- Son of Parvati (and Shiva); his elephant head resulted from Shiva beheading him and replacing it with an elephant's head
- Vehicle: a mouse (Mushika)
- Invoked at the start of any new venture
- Symbol: broken tusk (used to write the Mahabharata as dictated by Vyasa)

**Hanuman:**
- Monkey god; embodiment of devotion (bhakti), strength, courage, and selfless service
- Son of Vayu (wind god) and Anjana
- Central hero of the Ramayana; devoted servant of Rama
- Leapt across the ocean to Lanka, carried a mountain of healing herbs, set Lanka on fire with his burning tail

**Durga:**
- Warrior goddess; fierce form of Parvati
- Created by the combined energies of all the gods to defeat the buffalo demon **Mahishasura**
- Rides a lion/tiger; has multiple arms holding divine weapons
- Celebrated during **Navaratri/Durga Puja**

**Kali:**
- Fiercest form of the divine feminine; goddess of time, death, and liberation
- Dark-skinned, wild-haired, wearing a garland of skulls and a skirt of severed arms
- Standing on Shiva's chest (to calm her rampage)
- Tongue protruding; represents the destruction of ego and ignorance

**Lakshmi:**
- Goddess of wealth, fortune, love, beauty, prosperity
- Rises from the ocean during the Samudra Manthan
- Four arms representing dharma, artha, kama, moksha
- Seated on a lotus; elephants flank her
- Worshipped especially during **Diwali**

**Saraswati:**
- Goddess of knowledge, music, arts, speech, learning
- White-clad; plays the veena (stringed instrument)
- Holds sacred texts; prayer beads
- Vehicle: swan (hamsa), which can separate milk from water (discernment)

**Kartikeya/Murugan/Skanda:**
- God of war; commander of the divine army
- Six-headed (Shanmukha); rides a peacock
- Particularly important in South Indian tradition
- Defeated the demon Tarakasura

### The Ramayana

Attributed to the sage **Valmiki**; one of the two great Sanskrit epics.

**Summary:** Prince **Rama** of Ayodhya, avatar of Vishnu, is exiled to the forest for 14 years due to court intrigue (his stepmother Kaikeyi's demand). He goes with his wife **Sita** (avatar of Lakshmi) and brother **Lakshmana**. The demon king **Ravana** of Lanka abducts Sita. Rama allies with the monkey king **Sugriva** and the devoted **Hanuman**. A great army of monkeys and bears builds a bridge across the ocean to Lanka. Rama defeats Ravana in battle and rescues Sita. They return to Ayodhya for Rama's coronation. (In some versions, Sita undergoes a fire ordeal to prove her purity, and is later exiled to the forest when rumors persist — a section called *Uttara Kanda* whose authenticity is debated.)

**Themes:** Dharma (duty), ideal kingship, devotion, the triumph of good over evil, the cost of honor.

### The Mahabharata

The longest epic poem in the world (~100,000 verses); attributed to **Vyasa**.

**Central conflict:** Succession dispute between cousins — the **Pandavas** (five brothers: Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, Nakula, Sahadeva) and the **Kauravas** (100 brothers led by Duryodhana).

**Key events:**
- Draupadi's marriage to all five Pandavas (polyandry)
- The rigged dice game where the Pandavas lose everything, including Draupadi, who is humiliated in court (Draupadi's disrobing — Krishna miraculously extends her sari infinitely)
- 13 years of exile
- Failed peace negotiations
- The **Kurukshetra War** — 18-day devastating battle

**The Bhagavad Gita** ("Song of God"): Philosophical dialogue between **Arjuna** (who hesitates to fight his own relatives) and **Krishna** (his charioteer, who reveals himself as the Supreme Being). Discusses duty (dharma), paths of yoga (knowledge, devotion, action), the nature of the self (atman), and cosmic order. One of the most important texts in Hinduism.

**Aftermath:** The Pandavas win but at terrible cost. Most warriors on both sides perish. Yudhishthira becomes king. The Pandavas eventually renounce the world and journey to the Himalayas.

### Cosmology — Yugas and Lokas

**The Four Yugas (World Ages):**
1. **Satya Yuga (Krita Yuga)** — Golden Age; dharma at 100%; lasts 1,728,000 years
2. **Treta Yuga** — Silver Age; dharma at 75%; Rama's era; 1,296,000 years
3. **Dvapara Yuga** — Bronze Age; dharma at 50%; Krishna's era; 864,000 years
4. **Kali Yuga** — Iron/Dark Age; dharma at 25%; current age; 432,000 years; ends with Kalki avatar

One complete cycle (Mahayuga) = 4,320,000 years. 1,000 Mahayugas = one day of Brahma (kalpa). Brahma's lifespan = 311 trillion years; after which the universe is fully dissolved (Mahapralaya) before being recreated.

**The Lokas (Realms/Planes of Existence):**
- **Satyaloka** (Brahmaloka) — realm of Brahma; highest
- **Tapoloka** — realm of austerity
- **Janaloka** — realm of the spiritually advanced
- **Maharloka** — realm of great saints
- **Svargaloka** — heaven; realm of Indra and the devas
- **Bhuloka** — Earth; human realm
- **Atala, Vitala, Sutala, Talatala, Mahatala, Rasatala, Patala** — seven lower realms
- **Naraka** — hells (multiple, temporary; for karmic punishment)

### The Samudra Manthan (Churning of the Ocean of Milk)

A pivotal myth: The devas (gods) and asuras (demons) cooperated to churn the cosmic ocean using Mount Mandara as a churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as a rope. Vishnu as Kurma (tortoise) supported the mountain. From the ocean emerged:
- **Halahala** (deadly poison) — Shiva drank it, turning his throat blue
- **Lakshmi** — goddess of wealth
- **Kamadhenu** — wish-fulfilling cow
- **Kalpavriksha** — wish-fulfilling tree
- **Airavata** — Indra's white elephant
- **Apsaras** — celestial nymphs
- **Amrita** (nectar of immortality) — fought over by devas and asuras; Vishnu as Mohini (enchantress form) tricked the asuras

## 5. JAPANESE MYTHOLOGY (Shinto/Buddhist)

### Sources

**Kojiki** ("Record of Ancient Matters," 712 CE) and **Nihon Shoki** ("Chronicles of Japan," 720 CE) — primary sources for Shinto mythology. Buddhist traditions and Chinese influences also heavily shaped Japanese mythology. Folk traditions preserved in regional legends, Noh and Kabuki theater, and later literary works.

### Creation Myth — Izanagi and Izanami

From primordial chaos, the first gods (Kotoamatsukami) appeared, then seven generations of divine pairs. The last pair were **Izanagi** (he who invites) and **Izanami** (she who invites).

Standing on the Floating Bridge of Heaven, they stirred the cosmic ocean with a jeweled spear (Amenonuhoko). Drops falling from the spear formed the first island, **Onogoro**. They descended, performed a marriage ritual (circling a pillar), and gave birth to the Japanese islands and various kami (gods/spirits).

Izanami died giving birth to **Kagutsuchi** (fire god). Izanagi, grief-stricken, killed Kagutsuchi (whose blood and body created more kami) and traveled to **Yomi** (the underworld/land of the dead) to retrieve her. He found her rotting and fled in horror. Izanami, enraged, pursued him. He sealed the entrance to Yomi with a boulder. She vowed to kill 1,000 people daily; he vowed to create 1,500 (explaining why population grows).

Izanagi purified himself in a river, and from this purification were born three supreme kami:
- **Amaterasu** — from washing his left eye
- **Tsukuyomi** — from washing his right eye
- **Susanoo** — from washing his nose

### Major Deities (Kami)

| Kami | Domains | Key Myths |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Amaterasu** | Sun, universe, ruler of Takamagahara (High Plain of Heaven) | Most important Shinto deity; ancestor of the Imperial family; hid in a cave (Ama-no-Iwato) when Susanoo's rampage offended her, plunging the world into darkness; lured out by the other gods with the mirror Yata no Kagami and the dancing of Ame-no-Uzume |
| **Susanoo** | Storms, sea, valor | Banished from heaven for his destructive behavior; slew the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi to save Kushinadahime; found the sword Kusanagi no Tsurugi in its tail |
| **Tsukuyomi** | Moon | Killed the food goddess Uke Mochi, angering Amaterasu, who separated from him (explaining why sun and moon are never together) |
| **Inari** | Rice, foxes, fertility, tea, sake, industry, prosperity | Served by fox (kitsune) messengers; one of the most popular kami; gender-fluid |
| **Raijin** | Thunder, lightning | Depicted with drums surrounding him; often paired with Fujin |
| **Fujin** | Wind | Carries a bag of winds; one of the oldest Shinto deities |
| **Hachiman** | War, archery, divine protector of Japan | Syncretic Shinto-Buddhist deity; deified Emperor Ojin |
| **Ame-no-Uzume** | Dawn, mirth, revelry | Her ribald dance lured Amaterasu from the cave |
| **Okuninushi** | Nation-building, medicine, love | Son/descendant of Susanoo; created the land of Japan (Izumo); eventually ceded it to Amaterasu's descendants |
| **Benzaiten** | Water, music, knowledge, eloquence | One of the Seven Lucky Gods (Shichifukujin); originally the Hindu goddess Saraswati |

**Three Imperial Regalia of Japan:**
1. **Yata no Kagami** (mirror) — symbol of Amaterasu; honesty
2. **Kusanagi no Tsurugi** (sword) — found in Orochi; valor
3. **Yasakani no Magatama** (jewel) — benevolence

### Yokai and Supernatural Creatures

Yokai are supernatural beings, spirits, and monsters from Japanese folklore. They range from malevolent to mischievous to benign.

| Yokai | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Kitsune** | Fox spirits; can shapeshift (often into beautiful women); associated with Inari; gain more tails with age (up to nine); can be benevolent or tricksters |
| **Tanuki** | Raccoon dog spirits; shapeshifters; jovial, mischievous; associated with prosperity; depicted with large bellies and sake bottles |
| **Tengu** | Mountain spirits; originally bird-like (karasu tengu); later long-nosed red-faced humanoids (daitengu); martial arts masters; associated with yamabushi monks |
| **Oni** | Ogre-demons; usually red or blue; carry iron clubs (kanabo); associated with punishment in Buddhist hell |
| **Kappa** | River creatures; turtle-like with a dish of water on their head (source of power); fond of cucumbers; can be polite and can be dangerous; will drown swimmers |
| **Yurei** | Ghosts of the dead; typically women in white with long black hair; bound by strong emotions (vengeance, sorrow, love) |
| **Jorogumo** | Spider woman who seduces men |
| **Nekomata** | Cat demons with forked tails |
| **Gashadokuro** | Giant skeleton assembled from bones of those who died of starvation |
| **Yuki-onna** | Snow woman; beautiful pale woman who appears in snowstorms; can freeze victims |
| **Nure-onna** | Snake-bodied woman who appears at water's edge |
| **Tsuchigumo** | Giant earth spider |
| **Bakeneko / Nekomata** | Supernatural cats; shapeshifters |
| **Kodama** | Tree spirits |
| **Rokurokubi** | Women whose necks stretch at night |

### Key Legends

**Momotaro (Peach Boy):** A boy born from a giant peach, raised by an elderly couple; sets off to defeat the oni of Onigashima (Demon Island) with companions: a dog, a monkey, and a pheasant.

**Urashima Taro:** A fisherman who rescues a turtle, is taken to the Dragon Palace (Ryugu-jo) under the sea, stays for what seems like days, returns to find centuries have passed; opens a forbidden box and ages instantly.

**Kaguya-hime (The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter):** A tiny girl found inside a bamboo stalk grows into an extraordinarily beautiful woman; rejects many suitors (including the Emperor); revealed to be from the Moon; returns there. Considered the oldest Japanese narrative.

**Yamata no Orochi:** Susanoo's battle with the eight-headed, eight-tailed serpent. He tricked it by setting out eight vats of sake; when the heads drank and fell asleep, he slew it.

## 6. CHINESE MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

**Shan Hai Jing** (Classic of Mountains and Seas), **Huainanzi**, **Fengshen Yanyi** (Investiture of the Gods), **Journey to the West** (Xi You Ji), various historical chronicles, Taoist and Buddhist texts, folk traditions.

### Creation Myths

**Pangu and the Cosmic Egg:**
The universe began as a formless egg of chaos. **Pangu** grew inside it for 18,000 years. He broke the egg apart; the light yang elements rose to become the sky, and the heavy yin elements sank to become the earth. Pangu stood between them, growing taller daily, pushing them apart. After 18,000 more years, he died, and his body became the world:
- His breath became wind and clouds
- His voice became thunder
- His left eye became the sun, his right eye the moon
- His blood became rivers, his flesh became soil
- His hair became stars and vegetation
- His sweat became rain
- The parasites on his body became humanity (in some versions)

**Nuwa Creates Humanity:**
**Nuwa**, a serpent-bodied goddess, felt lonely on earth and created humans from yellow clay. She carefully sculpted the first ones (who became nobles) but grew tired and swung a rope through mud, flinging droplets that became common people. She also repaired the sky when the pillar holding it up was broken (patching it with five-colored stones, cutting off the legs of a giant turtle to serve as new pillars).

**Fuxi and Nuwa:**
Fuxi (sometimes Nuwa's brother/husband) is credited with inventing writing, fishing, trapping, and the eight trigrams (bagua) of the I Ching. They are often depicted as human above the waist with intertwined serpent tails below.

### Major Deities

| Deity | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Jade Emperor (Yu Huang)** | Ruler of Heaven and all realms | Supreme deity of the Taoist pantheon; governs the celestial bureaucracy |
| **Queen Mother of the West (Xi Wangmu)** | Immortality, punishment, plague | Resides on Mount Kunlun; keeper of the Peaches of Immortality |
| **Nuwa** | Creation, reproduction, repairing the sky | Serpent-bodied creator goddess |
| **Pangu** | Creation | Primordial being who separated yin and yang |
| **Fuxi** | Civilization, trigrams, fishing | First of the Three Sovereigns |
| **Shennong** | Agriculture, medicine | Divine Farmer; tasted hundreds of herbs; invented the plow |
| **Huangdi (Yellow Emperor)** | Civilization, culture | Legendary first emperor; ancestor of the Chinese people; defeated Chi You |
| **Guan Yu** | War, righteousness, loyalty, brotherhood | Historical figure (d. 220 CE) deified; "God of War"; red-faced with long beard; one of the most popular deities |
| **Mazu** | Sea, sailors | Protector of fishermen and seafarers; especially important in coastal and Taiwanese communities |
| **Zhong Kui** | Vanquisher of ghosts and evil spirits | Fierce-looking scholar who failed the civil examination; became king of ghosts |
| **Chang'e** | Moon | Swallowed the elixir of immortality and floated to the moon; lives there with the Jade Rabbit |
| **Hou Yi** | Archery | Shot down nine of ten suns to save humanity from burning; husband of Chang'e |
| **Erlang Shen** | War, truth-seeing | Nephew of the Jade Emperor; has a third truth-seeing eye; accompanied by the Howling Celestial Dog |
| **Nezha** | Protection | Child warrior god; born as a ball of flesh; fought the Dragon Kings |

### Dragons, Phoenixes, and Mythical Creatures

**Chinese Dragons (Long):** Unlike Western dragons, Chinese dragons are benevolent, wise, and associated with water, rain, rivers, and imperial power. They are typically depicted as long, serpentine, with antlers, whiskers, and claws.

- **Azure Dragon (Qinglong)** — one of the Four Symbols; represents East and Spring
- **Dragon Kings** — four brothers ruling the four seas (East, West, North, South); can control weather
- Dragons had five claws (imperial symbol; four claws for nobility, three for common depictions)

**Fenghuang (Phoenix):** Represents virtue, grace, and the union of yin and yang. Associated with the empress (dragon = emperor). Not a fire bird (unlike the Western phoenix) but a symbol of harmony and prosperity.

**Four Auspicious Beasts:** Dragon, Phoenix, Qilin (chimeric creature symbolizing prosperity and serenity; sometimes translated "unicorn"), and Tortoise (often shown with a snake — the Black Tortoise/Xuanwu).

**Other Creatures:**
- **Qilin** — chimeric beast; appears during the reign of a just ruler; walks on grass without bending it
- **Pixiu** — lion-like creature that devours gold and silver; protector of wealth
- **Baihu (White Tiger)** — one of the Four Symbols; represents West and Autumn
- **Hundun** — chaos incarnate; faceless being
- **Jiangshi** — hopping vampires/reanimated corpses
- **Fox spirits (Huli Jing)** — similar to Japanese kitsune; shapeshifters, often seductive women; nine-tailed fox (most powerful)

### Journey to the West (Xi You Ji)

One of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, attributed to **Wu Cheng'en** (16th century). A fictionalized account of the Buddhist monk **Xuanzang's** pilgrimage to India to obtain sacred sutras.

**Main Characters:**
- **Sun Wukong (Monkey King)** — born from a stone; learns immortality and 72 transformations; acquires the Ruyi Jingu Bang (size-changing iron staff) from the Dragon King; wreaks havoc in Heaven; imprisoned under a mountain by the Buddha for 500 years; freed to accompany Xuanzang; represents the untamed mind gradually disciplined through spiritual journey
- **Tang Sanzang (Tripitaka/Xuanzang)** — the Buddhist monk; reincarnation of a divine being; pious but naive
- **Zhu Bajie (Pigsy)** — former celestial marshal reborn as a pig-man; gluttonous and lustful but loyal; represents earthly desires
- **Sha Wujing (Sandy)** — former celestial general; quiet, loyal, steadfast; river ogre
- **Bai Longma** — the White Dragon Horse; actually a dragon prince; serves as Xuanzang's mount

They face 81 tribulations on their journey, battling demons and overcoming obstacles. The story blends Taoist, Buddhist, and folk elements and is enormously influential across East Asian culture.

### The Eight Immortals (Ba Xian)

Eight legendary Taoist figures who achieved immortality, each representing a different condition of life:

| Immortal | Represents | Symbol |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **He Xiangu** | Women | Lotus flower |
| **Cao Guojiu** | Nobility | Castanets/jade board |
| **Li Tieguai** | Sick/disabled | Iron crutch, gourd |
| **Lan Caihe** | Youth | Flower basket |
| **Lu Dongbin** | Scholars | Sword, fly-whisk |
| **Han Xiangzi** | Culture | Flute |
| **Zhang Guolao** | Old age | Paper donkey, drum |
| **Zhongli Quan** | Military | Fan |

They crossed the sea using their individual powers (not a boat) — the story "Eight Immortals Crossing the Sea" symbolizes using one's unique strengths.

## 7. CELTIC MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

Irish and Welsh literary traditions preserved Celtic myths. Key Irish texts: **Lebor Gabala Erenn** (Book of Invasions), **Cath Maige Tuired** (Battle of Moytura). Welsh: **Mabinogi/Mabinogion**. Also continental Celtic (Gaulish) archaeological evidence and Greek/Roman accounts.

### The Four Cycles of Irish Mythology

**1. Mythological Cycle:**
Deals with the successive invasions of Ireland and the gods (Tuatha De Danann).

**Book of Invasions (Lebor Gabala Erenn):** Ireland was settled by six successive groups:
- Cessair (pre-flood)
- Partholon
- Nemed
- Fir Bolg (established the five provinces)
- **Tuatha De Danann** (the gods)
- **Milesians** (ancestors of the Irish; defeated the Tuatha De Danann, who retreated into the **sidhe** — hollow hills/fairy mounds — becoming the **Aos Si/Aes Sidhe** ("people of the mounds," i.e., the fairies of later Irish folklore))

**2. Ulster Cycle (Red Branch Cycle):**
Set around the 1st century CE in Ulster. Centers on King **Conchobar mac Nessa** and the warriors of the Red Branch.

- **Cu Chulainn** — greatest warrior of the Ulster Cycle; son of Lugh (or mortal Sualtam) and Dechtire. His birth name was Setanta; earned the name Cu Chulainn ("Hound of Culann") after killing a guard dog and offering to take its place. Known for his **riastrad** (warp spasm) — a terrifying battle frenzy that transformed his body. Wielded the **Gae Bolga** (barbed spear). Died young (tied himself to a standing stone so he would die on his feet). Central tale: **Tain Bo Cuailnge** (The Cattle Raid of Cooley) — Queen **Medb** of Connacht invades Ulster to steal the bull Donn Cuailnge; Cu Chulainn single-handedly defends Ulster while its warriors are incapacitated by a curse.
- **Deirdre of the Sorrows** — tragic love story; Deirdre was prophesied to bring ruin; she fled with Naoise rather than marry King Conchobar; Conchobar had Naoise killed; she died of grief.

**3. Fenian Cycle (Ossianic Cycle):**
Stories of **Fionn mac Cumhaill** (Finn McCool) and the **Fianna** (warrior band).
- Fionn gained supernatural wisdom by tasting the Salmon of Knowledge (Bradán Feasa)
- His son **Oisin** traveled to **Tir na nOg** (Land of the Young) with the fairy woman Niamh; returned to find centuries had passed and he aged to an old man upon touching the ground
- Other key figures: **Diarmuid** (the love spot; tragic romance with Grainne), **Oscar** (Oisin's son)

**4. Historical Cycle (Cycle of the Kings):**
Stories of historical and semi-historical Irish kings, blending history with myth.

### Tuatha De Danann — Key Figures

| Deity | Domains | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **The Dagda** | Earth, abundance, magic, wisdom, druidry | "The Good God" (good at everything); wields an enormous club (kills with one end, resurrects with the other); owns an inexhaustible cauldron and a magical harp |
| **Lugh** (Lamfada, "Long Arm") | Light, crafts, skill, war | Master of all arts; slew his grandfather Balor of the Evil Eye at the Second Battle of Moytura; father of Cu Chulainn |
| **The Morrigan** | War, fate, death, sovereignty | Triple goddess (Morrigan, Badb, Macha — or various combinations); appears as a crow/raven on the battlefield; shapeshifter; prophesied the end of the world |
| **Brigid** | Healing, poetry, smithcraft, spring | Daughter of the Dagda; later syncretized with St. Brigid; her festival is Imbolc (February 1) |
| **Dian Cecht** | Healing, medicine | Chief physician; made a silver hand for Nuada |
| **Nuada** (Airgetlam, "Silver Hand") | Kingship, justice | First king of the Tuatha De Danann; lost his hand in battle (replaced with silver); had to abdicate (kings must be unblemished) |
| **Goibniu** | Smithing | Divine smith; weapons he made never missed; brewed the ale of immortality |
| **Manannan mac Lir** | Sea, weather, Otherworld | Ruler of Mag Mell; owns a magical boat, horse, and cloak of invisibility; foster father of Lugh |
| **Aengus Og** | Love, youth, poetic inspiration | Son of the Dagda; won the Brug na Boinne (Newgrange) through a trick |
| **Balor** | Destruction | Fomorian king; his evil eye destroyed all it gazed upon; killed by his grandson Lugh with a sling stone |

### The Otherworld

Celtic mythology features multiple Otherworld concepts, all lands of supernatural beauty, plenty, and immortality:
- **Tir na nOg** — Land of the Young
- **Mag Mell** — Plain of Delight
- **Tech Duinn** — House of Donn (realm of the dead; where the dead gather)
- **Annwn** (Welsh) — the Otherworld; ruled by Arawn
- The Otherworld was accessible through fairy mounds (sidhe), across the western sea, through lakes, at liminal times (Samhain, Beltane)
- Time moves differently there (a few days = centuries in the mortal world)

### Welsh Mythology (Mabinogion)

**Four Branches of the Mabinogi:** Stories of Pwyll, Branwen, Manawydan, and Math.

Key figures:
- **Pwyll** — Lord of Dyfed; encounters Arawn, Lord of Annwn
- **Rhiannon** — divine horse goddess; falsely accused of killing her son Pryderi
- **Bran the Blessed (Bendigeidfran)** — giant king of Britain; his severed head continued speaking and protected the land
- **Arianrhod** — sky/star goddess; mother of Lleu Llaw Gyffes (Welsh counterpart of Lugh)
- **Gwydion** — trickster magician
- **Blodeuwedd** — woman made from flowers; turned into an owl for infidelity and attempted murder

## 8. MESOPOTAMIAN MYTHOLOGY (Sumerian/Babylonian/Assyrian)

### Sources

**Sumerian:** Earliest known written mythology (c. 3rd millennium BCE). Clay tablets with cuneiform writing from Uruk, Ur, Nippur, Eridu, and other cities.
**Babylonian:** **Enuma Elish** (creation epic), **Epic of Gilgamesh** (c. 2100 BCE origin, standard version c. 1200 BCE), **Atrahasis** (flood story), **Descent of Ishtar**.
**Assyrian:** Largely adopted Babylonian mythology with some variations.

### Major Deities

| Deity | Sumerian Name | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------------|--------|-------|
| **Anu** | An | Sky, king of the gods, heaven | Supreme authority; father of many gods; rarely intervened directly |
| **Enlil** | Enlil | Wind, air, storms, earth | Lord of the universe; determined destinies; decreed the Great Flood; wielder of the Tablets of Destiny |
| **Enki** | Ea | Fresh water, wisdom, magic, crafts, civilization | Trickster-sage; warned Utnapishtim of the flood; created humanity from clay; patron of the city Eridu; lived in the Abzu (subterranean freshwater ocean) |
| **Ishtar** | Inanna | Love, war, fertility, sex, power | Most important goddess; Queen of Heaven; descended to the Underworld; complex and formidable; associated with the planet Venus |
| **Marduk** | — | Supreme god (Babylonian); storm, creation, magic | Patron of Babylon; hero of the Enuma Elish; slew Tiamat; created the world from her body; rose to supremacy as Babylon's power grew |
| **Shamash** | Utu | Sun, justice, truth | Judge of heaven and earth; gave laws to Hammurabi |
| **Sin** | Nanna | Moon | Father of Shamash and Ishtar; patron of Ur |
| **Tiamat** | — | Primordial salt water, chaos | Monstrous goddess of the sea; created eleven monsters; slain by Marduk; her body became heaven and earth |
| **Apsu** | Abzu | Primordial fresh water | Consort of Tiamat; killed by Enki; his waters became the underground freshwater ocean |
| **Ereshkigal** | Ereshkigal | Queen of the Underworld | Sister of Ishtar; rules Kur/Irkalla (the land of the dead) with husband Nergal |
| **Nergal** | Erra | War, plague, death, underworld | Became king of the underworld by marrying Ereshkigal |
| **Dumuzi** | Tammuz | Shepherds, vegetation | Husband/consort of Inanna; sent to the underworld as her substitute; dies and is reborn seasonally (origin of dying-and-rising god archetype) |
| **Ningishzida** | — | Vegetation, underworld | Often depicted with serpents |
| **Nisaba** | — | Writing, grain, wisdom | Goddess of writing before Nabu took prominence |

### The Enuma Elish (Babylonian Creation Epic)

1. In the beginning: **Apsu** (fresh water) and **Tiamat** (salt water) mingled, giving birth to the gods.
2. The younger gods were noisy and disturbed Apsu, who plotted to destroy them.
3. **Enki** killed Apsu and established his dwelling on Apsu's waters.
4. Tiamat, enraged, created eleven monstrous creatures (including serpent-dragons, scorpion-men, and fish-men) and appointed **Kingu** as their leader, giving him the Tablets of Destiny.
5. No god dared face Tiamat until **Marduk** volunteered, on the condition that he be made supreme god.
6. Marduk slew Tiamat with winds and an arrow, split her body in two: one half became the sky, the other the earth.
7. He created the calendar, organized the cosmos, and fashioned humanity from the blood of Kingu (so that humans would serve the gods and free them from labor).
8. The gods built Babylon and the temple Esagila in Marduk's honor.
9. Marduk was proclaimed supreme by all gods, given fifty names of power.

### The Epic of Gilgamesh

The oldest great work of literature. Gilgamesh was a historical king of Uruk (c. 2700 BCE) who became legendary.

**Summary:**
- **Gilgamesh**, two-thirds divine and one-third mortal, is king of Uruk but is arrogant and oppressive.
- The gods create **Enkidu**, a wild man, as his equal. After a contest, they become inseparable friends.
- Together they slay **Humbaba** (guardian of the Cedar Forest) and the **Bull of Heaven** (sent by Ishtar after Gilgamesh rejects her advances).
- The gods decree Enkidu must die as punishment. His death devastates Gilgamesh.
- Terrified of mortality, Gilgamesh embarks on a quest for eternal life.
- He reaches **Utnapishtim** (the Mesopotamian Noah), who tells him the story of the **Great Flood** (remarkably similar to the later Biblical account of Noah) — how Enki warned him to build a boat, load it with animals and family, survived the flood, and was granted immortality by the gods.
- Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh of a plant at the bottom of the sea that restores youth. Gilgamesh retrieves it, but a serpent steals it while he bathes.
- Gilgamesh returns to Uruk, accepting that his immortality lies in the great walls he built and his legacy.

**Themes:** Mortality, friendship, the meaning of civilization, the limits of human ambition, the quest for meaning in the face of death.

### The Descent of Inanna/Ishtar

Inanna descends to the Underworld (Kur), ruled by her sister Ereshkigal. At each of seven gates, she must remove one article of clothing/power (a striptease of divine authority). Naked and powerless, she is killed by Ereshkigal and hung on a hook.

Enki rescues her by sending beings to sprinkle the water and food of life on her. But the rule of the Underworld demands a substitute: Inanna sends her husband **Dumuzi** in her place (angry that he wasn't mourning her). Eventually, a compromise: Dumuzi spends half the year in the Underworld, his sister Geshtinanna the other half (a seasonal myth similar to Persephone).

### The Underworld (Kur/Irkalla)

- A dark, dusty realm where the dead exist as shadows, eating dust and clay
- "The land of no return"
- No punishment or reward — all dead go there regardless of how they lived (contrast with Egyptian afterlife)
- Seven gates, each requiring surrender of something
- Ruled by Ereshkigal and Nergal

## 9. AFRICAN MYTHOLOGY

African mythology encompasses hundreds of distinct traditions. Here are some of the most significant:

### Yoruba Mythology (Nigeria, Benin, Togo)

One of the richest and most influential African mythological systems, spread to the Americas through the African diaspora (becoming Candomble in Brazil, Santeria in Cuba, Vodou influences in Haiti).

**Cosmology:**
- **Olodumare/Olorun** — supreme creator God; remote and transcendent; delegated creation to subordinate deities
- **Orishas** — divine spirits/deities; intermediaries between humans and Olodumare; each orisha governs specific aspects of nature and human life

**Key Orishas:**

| Orisha | Domain | Symbols/Attributes |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Obatala** | Creation of human bodies, purity, peace, wisdom | White cloth; molder of human forms from clay (some imperfections from drinking palm wine explain disabilities) |
| **Ogun** | Iron, war, metalworking, technology, justice | Iron, machete, dog; patron of blacksmiths, warriors, and modern technology |
| **Shango (Sango)** | Thunder, lightning, fire, justice, dance | Double-headed axe (oshe), ram; historical king of Oyo deified; charismatic and powerful |
| **Oshun** | Sweet water, love, fertility, beauty, prosperity, diplomacy | River water, honey, gold, peacock, mirror; associated with the Oshun River |
| **Yemoja (Yemanja)** | Ocean, motherhood, children, fertility | Ocean, fish, seashells, crescent moon; mother of many orishas |
| **Eshu/Elegba** | Trickster, crossroads, communication, fate, chance | Crossroads; mediator between humans and orishas; must be propitiated first in any ritual; neither good nor evil but essential |
| **Orunmila/Ifa** | Divination, wisdom, destiny | Palm nuts; the Ifa divination system is among the most complex in the world |
| **Oya** | Wind, storms, transformation, death, rebirth | Buffalo, copper, lightning; guards the gates of cemeteries |
| **Osanyin** | Herbal medicine, healing, the forest | One-legged figure; knowledge of plants |
| **Shopona/Babaluaye** | Disease (especially smallpox), healing | Feared and respected; associated with epidemics |

**Creation Myth:** Olodumare sent Obatala down from heaven on a chain with a calabash containing earth, a five-toed chicken, and a palm nut. Obatala poured the earth on the primordial waters and the chicken scratched it to spread it, creating land. The palm nut grew into a tree. The place where this happened became Ile-Ife, the sacred city. Obatala molded humans from clay; Olodumare breathed life into them.

### Akan Mythology (Ghana, Cote d'Ivoire)

**Anansi** — the spider trickster; one of the most famous African mythological figures. Son of Nyame (sky god) and Asase Ya (earth goddess).

Key stories:
- Anansi captured the python, hornets, leopard, and fairy to purchase all stories from Nyame (origin of why all folk tales are called "Anansi stories")
- Uses cunning rather than strength
- Spread to the Caribbean and American South through the African diaspora (became "Brer Rabbit" via cultural adaptation)

**Nyame** — supreme sky god
**Asase Ya** — earth goddess

### Zulu Mythology (Southern Africa)

- **Unkulunkulu** — the supreme creator; "the Old Old One"; emerged from a reed bed
- **Nomkhubulwane** — goddess of rain, agriculture, fertility; rainbow goddess
- **uMvelinqangi** — "the one who was there before everything"
- **Mamlambo** — a river goddess; brain-sucker monster (also reflects later colonial-era folklore additions)
- Ancestor veneration is central; the **amadlozi** (ancestral spirits) guide and protect
- The **Tokoloshe** — a mischievous or malevolent dwarf-like creature summoned by witches

### Maasai Mythology (Kenya, Tanzania)

- **Enkai/Engai** — supreme god; two aspects: Engai Narok (benevolent, black god of rain) and Engai Na-nyokie (angry, red god)
- The Maasai believe they are the chosen people of Enkai
- Origin myth: Enkai sent cattle down from the sky on a leather thong to the first Maasai; this is why they believe all cattle belong to them
- **Olapa** — moon goddess; married to Enkai; her wounding (explaining moon phases) came from a quarrel

### Other African Creation Myths

**Dogon (Mali):** Complex cosmology involving **Amma** (creator god) who created twin Nommo spirits. Dogon astronomical knowledge (awareness of Sirius B, a companion star invisible to the naked eye) has been a subject of much debate.

**Bushongo/Kuba (Central Africa):** **Bumba** — the creator who vomited up the sun, moon, stars, animals, and humans.

**Fon (Dahomey/Benin):** **Mawu-Lisa** — androgynous or twin creator deities (Mawu: moon, female; Lisa: sun, male). **Dan/Damballa** — the cosmic serpent that holds the world together.

## 10. MESOAMERICAN MYTHOLOGY

### Aztec Mythology

**Sources:** Post-conquest documents (Codex Borgia, Codex Borbonicus, Florentine Codex compiled by Bernardino de Sahagun), surviving pre-conquest codices, archaeological evidence.

**Cosmology — The Five Suns:**
The Aztecs believed the current world was the fifth creation. Each previous world (Sun) was destroyed:

| Sun | Presiding God | Destruction |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| **Nahui Ocelotl** (4 Jaguar) | Tezcatlipoca | Jaguars devoured humanity |
| **Nahui Ehecatl** (4 Wind) | Quetzalcoatl | Hurricanes; humans became monkeys |
| **Nahui Quiahuitl** (4 Rain) | Tlaloc | Rain of fire; humans became birds |
| **Nahui Atl** (4 Water) | Chalchiuhtlicue | Great flood; humans became fish |
| **Nahui Ollin** (4 Movement) | Tonatiuh | Current age; will end in earthquakes |

**Major Deities:**

| Deity | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Quetzalcoatl** ("Feathered Serpent") | Wind, learning, creation, priesthood, Venus (morning star) | One of the most important Mesoamerican deities; appeared across cultures; creator of humanity (retrieved bones from Mictlan and anointed them with his blood); opposed human sacrifice in some traditions; associated with the legend of his departure and prophesied return |
| **Tezcatlipoca** ("Smoking Mirror") | Night, sorcery, destiny, conflict, rulers, jaguars | Quetzalcoatl's rival/counterpart; associated with obsidian mirrors; patron of warriors and sorcerers; "Lord of the Near and the Nigh" |
| **Huitzilopochtli** | Sun, war | Patron god of the Aztecs and Tenochtitlan; born fully formed from his mother Coatlicue; killed his sister Coyolxauhqui (moon) and 400 brothers (stars); required human sacrifice to fuel the sun |
| **Tlaloc** | Rain, water, fertility, agriculture | Goggle-eyed rain god; benevolent (bringing rain) and destructive (floods, drought); children who drowned went to his paradise Tlalocan; one of the oldest Mesoamerican deities |
| **Coatlicue** ("She of the Serpent Skirt") | Earth, motherhood, death | Mother of Huitzilopochtli; terrifying appearance (skirt of serpents, necklace of hearts and hands, skull face) |
| **Xipe Totec** ("Our Lord the Flayed One") | Spring, renewal, goldsmiths | Priests wore the flayed skins of sacrificial victims (symbolizing the earth putting on a new "skin" in spring) |
| **Mictlantecuhtli** | Death, lord of Mictlan (underworld) | Skeletal figure; ruled the lowest level of the underworld |
| **Tlazolteotl** | Purification, filth, sin, lust | Goddess who consumed sins during confession |
| **Tonatiuh** | Sun (current Sun) | Demands blood sacrifice to continue moving |

**The Aztec Underworld (Mictlan):**
Nine levels that the dead must traverse over four years, facing trials:
- Crossing a wide river with a dog's help (dogs were buried with the dead)
- Passing between clashing mountains
- Climbing an obsidian mountain
- Crossing a wind of knives
- Arriving before Mictlantecuhtli and Mictecacihuatl

Warriors who died in battle went to the **Eastern Paradise** (accompanying the sun); women who died in childbirth went to the **Western Paradise**; those who drowned went to **Tlalocan** (Tlaloc's paradise).

### Maya Mythology

**Sources:** **Popol Vuh** (K'iche' Maya creation narrative), **Chilam Balam**, surviving codices (Dresden, Madrid, Paris, Grolier), temple inscriptions, painted ceramics.

**Key Deities:**

| Deity | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Itzamna** | Creation, writing, wisdom, sky | Supreme creator god; inventor of writing and calendars; benevolent |
| **Kukulkan** (K'uk'ulkan) | Feathered serpent | Maya equivalent of Quetzalcoatl; associated with wind and learning |
| **Ix Chel** | Moon, medicine, weaving, childbirth | "Lady Rainbow"; depicted as both a young beauty and a fearsome old woman with a serpent crown |
| **Chaac** | Rain, lightning | Long-nosed rain god; similar to Tlaloc; wielded an axe (lightning) |
| **Ah Puch / Kisin** | Death, lord of Xibalba | Skeletal death god; ruled the underworld |
| **Kinich Ahau** | Sun | "Sun-faced Lord"; the sun god |
| **Bolon Tzacab / K'awiil** | Lightning, royalty, sustenance | Often depicted with a serpent leg; symbol of royal power |

**The Popol Vuh:**
The K'iche' Maya creation narrative and epic. Key elements:

**Creation:** The gods (Heart of Sky, Sovereign Plumed Serpent) attempted to create beings who would worship them:
1. First attempt: animals — could not speak or worship
2. Second: mud people — dissolved
3. Third: wooden people — had no souls or hearts; destroyed by a flood and attacks by their own tools and animals
4. Fourth: **corn people** — made from white and yellow maize; successful; the current humanity

**The Hero Twins — Hunahpu and Xbalanque:**
The most important Maya epic narrative:
- Their father and uncle (Hun Hunahpu and Vucub Hunahpu) played ball and disturbed the lords of **Xibalba** (the underworld). They were summoned, tricked, and killed.
- Hun Hunahpu's severed head was placed in a calabash tree. It spat into the hand of **Xquic** (Blood Moon), impregnating her with the twins.
- Hunahpu and Xbalanque grew up as clever tricksters and ballplayers.
- They descended to Xibalba and outwitted the death gods through a series of trials in the Houses of Darkness, Cold, Jaguars, Bats, and Fire.
- They allowed themselves to be killed, then were resurrected. They tricked the lords of Xibalba into asking to be sacrificed and dismembered — but did not resurrect them.
- They ascended to become the Sun and Moon (or the Sun and Venus).

**Xibalba (Maya Underworld):**
"Place of Fright." Ruled by death gods. Reached via a ballcourt or caves. Had multiple houses of torment (Razor House, Cold House, Jaguar House, Bat House, Fire House). Souls of the dead were tested and often failed.

## 11. POLYNESIAN MYTHOLOGY

### Cosmology and Creation

Polynesian myths share common elements across a vast oceanic region (Hawaii, New Zealand, Samoa, Tonga, Tahiti, Easter Island, etc.) but with local variations.

**Creation:** Many traditions begin with **Te Kore** (the Void/Nothingness), then **Te Po** (the Darkness/Night), then **Te Ao** (the Light/World).

In Maori tradition: **Ranginui** (Sky Father) and **Papatuanuku** (Earth Mother) were locked in a tight embrace, their children trapped between them in darkness. Their sons debated what to do. **Tane** (god of forests) pushed them apart — Rangi above, Papa below — bringing light to the world. **Tawhirimatea** (storm god) was angry and attacked his brothers, causing storms. The weeping of Rangi (rain) and Papa (mist) continues their longing for each other.

### Key Deities

| Deity | Domain | Tradition |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Tangaroa/Tagaloa/Kanaloa** | Ocean, sea creatures, creation | Pan-Polynesian; supreme creator in some traditions (Samoa); in Hawaiian tradition, Kanaloa is paired with Kane |
| **Tane/Kane** | Forests, birds, light, creation of humanity | Maori: separated sky and earth; created the first woman from earth; Hawaiian: Kane is supreme creator god |
| **Tu/Ku** | War | Maori: Tu Matauenga; Hawaiian: Ku; war god requiring sacrifice |
| **Rongo/Lono** | Agriculture, peace, fertility | Hawaiian: Lono associated with the Makahiki festival |
| **Tawhirimatea** | Weather, storms | Maori: rebelled against the separation of earth and sky |
| **Pele** | Volcanoes, fire, lightning | Hawaiian goddess; lives in Kilauea; passionate, jealous, powerful; her battles with her sister Namakaokahai created the island chain |
| **Maui** | Trickster/demigod | Pan-Polynesian culture hero (see below) |
| **Hina/Sina** | Moon, femininity | Various forms across Polynesia; often Maui's mother or companion |
| **Whiro** | Darkness, evil, death | Maori: lord of the underworld; enemy of Tane |

### Maui — The Great Polynesian Trickster/Demigod

Maui is the most famous Polynesian mythological figure, known across virtually all Polynesian traditions. He is a classic trickster-hero:

- **Fished up islands:** Using a magical fishhook (made from his grandmother's jawbone in some versions), Maui pulled up the North Island of New Zealand (Te Ika a Maui — "the fish of Maui"), and in Hawaiian tradition, tried to pull the islands together
- **Snared the sun:** The sun moved too fast across the sky. Maui captured it with ropes and beat it, forcing it to move more slowly (explaining longer days)
- **Stole fire:** Obtained the secret of fire from Mahuika (fire goddess/ancestress) for humanity
- **Died attempting immortality:** In Maori tradition, Maui tried to achieve immortality by entering the body of **Hine-nui-te-po** (goddess of death) through her birth passage while she slept, planning to emerge from her mouth. A bird laughed and woke her; she crushed him. Thus death remained permanent.

### Navigation Mythology

Polynesian navigation across the Pacific was extraordinary and mythologized:
- Stars, ocean swells, bird patterns, and cloud formations guided navigation
- **Kupe** — legendary Maori navigator who discovered New Zealand (Aotearoa)
- The great canoe voyages were central to identity
- **Hawaiki** — the legendary ancestral homeland from which Polynesians migrated

## 12. SLAVIC MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

Unlike Greek or Norse mythology, Slavic mythology was not systematically recorded before Christianization. Sources include: the **Primary Chronicle** (Nestor, 12th century), **Hypatian Codex**, folk songs, fairy tales collected by Alexander Afanasyev, folk practices, place names, comparative mythology, and chronicles by outsiders (Procopius, Thietmar of Merseburg, Helmold of Bosau).

### Cosmology

The Slavic cosmos was often envisioned as a **World Tree** (similar to Yggdrasil):
- **Upper realm (Prav)** — realm of the gods, order, truth
- **Middle realm (Yav)** — the visible, mortal world
- **Lower realm (Nav)** — realm of the dead, darkness, chaos

### Major Deities

| Deity | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Perun** | Thunder, lightning, war, justice, sky, oak | Supreme god of the Slavic pantheon; equivalent to Thor/Zeus; wielded an axe or hammer; his sacred tree was the oak; in eternal conflict with Veles |
| **Veles** | Underworld, cattle, wealth, magic, water, music, trickery | Perun's adversary; serpentine/dragon form; dwelt in the roots of the World Tree; a complex, ambivalent god of the "wet" world below; patron of poets and musicians |
| **Svarog** | Sky, fire, blacksmithing | Father of Dazhbog (or Svarozhich); celestial smith |
| **Dazhbog** | Sun, prosperity, giving | "Giving God"; solar deity; ancestor of the Slavic people in some traditions |
| **Mokosh** | Earth, fertility, women's work, spinning, weaving, fate | Only major female deity in the pantheon of Vladimir I; protector of women; associated with moisture and sheep; survived as folk saint "Paraskeva Pyatnitsa" |
| **Stribog** | Wind, air | Grandfather of the winds |
| **Khors** | Sun (disk) | Solar deity, possibly of Iranian origin; the sun's physical disk |
| **Simargl** | Seed, vegetation | Winged dog or bird; guardian of seeds and crops |
| **Marzanna/Morana** | Winter, death, rebirth | Effigies burned or drowned at the end of winter in folk rituals |
| **Jarilo/Yarilo** | Spring, fertility, vegetation | Young god of spring; dies and is reborn seasonally |
| **Rod** | Fate, ancestor, creation | Possibly a primordial creator god; associated with birth and fate; "rozhanitsy" were female fate spirits |

**The Perun-Veles Conflict:** The central myth of Slavic mythology. Veles steals something precious from Perun (cattle, his wife, or his son). Perun pursues Veles across the sky, striking him with lightning. Veles hides behind trees, rocks, and people (explaining why lightning strikes these). Eventually Veles is struck down and retreats to the underworld. This cycle repeats — a seasonal myth explaining storms and the cycle of seasons.

### Creatures and Spirits

| Creature | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Baba Yaga** | Fearsome old witch who lives in a hut on chicken legs in the deep forest; flies in a mortar, steers with a pestle, sweeps her tracks with a broom; can be helpful or deadly; tests heroes; guardian of the boundary between the living world and the dead |
| **Firebird (Zhar-Ptitsa)** | Glowing magical bird with feathers of gold and flame; finding a single feather brings both fortune and misfortune; the quest for the Firebird is a common fairy tale motif |
| **Leshy (Leshii)** | Forest spirit; protector of the forest and its animals; can change size; leads travelers astray; can be propitiated; often depicted as a tall figure with bark-like skin |
| **Rusalka** | Water spirit; ghost of a drowned maiden (or unbaptized child); beautiful and dangerous; lures men to watery deaths; associated with unfinished life business |
| **Vodyanoy** | Male water spirit; lives in rivers, lakes, mills; drowns swimmers; can be appeased with offerings |
| **Domovoy** | House spirit; protector of the household; can be benevolent or mischievous; lives behind the stove or under the threshold |
| **Kikimora** | Female household spirit; if good (Mokosh's servant), helps with housework; if bad, causes nightmares and disruption |
| **Zmey (Zmei Gorynych)** | Multi-headed dragon; often three, seven, or twelve heads; breathes fire; abducts maidens; slain by bogatyrs (heroes) |
| **Koschei the Deathless (Kashchei Bessmertnyi)** | Immortal villain; his death is hidden in a needle, inside an egg, inside a duck, inside a hare, inside a chest, buried under an oak on the island Buyan; abducts women |

### Heroes (Bogatyrs)

- **Ilya Muromets** — greatest Russian bogatyr; paralyzed for 33 years before gaining miraculous strength; defeated Nightingale the Robber; defender of Kiev
- **Dobrynya Nikitich** — dragon-slayer; diplomat and musician; defeated Zmey Gorynych
- **Alyosha Popovich** — the youngest and cleverest; relies on cunning rather than strength

## 13. NATIVE AMERICAN MYTHOLOGY

Native American mythology comprises hundreds of distinct traditions. Here are key shared themes and specific examples.

### Shared Themes

**Trickster Figures:**
- **Coyote** — most widespread trickster across Western and Plains nations; simultaneously creator, fool, culture hero, and agent of chaos. In many stories, Coyote steals fire for humanity, shapes the land, but also causes death and suffering through foolishness or selfishness.
- **Raven** — Pacific Northwest trickster and creator; stole the sun and released it from a box (bringing light to the world); Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian traditions
- **Iktomi** — Lakota spider trickster
- **Nanabozho/Nanabush** — Anishinaabe (Ojibwe) trickster/culture hero; sometimes a rabbit

**Thunderbird:**
A massive supernatural bird whose wings create thunder and whose eyes flash lightning. Widespread across many nations (Lakota, Ojibwe, Kwakwaka'wakw, and others). Often in conflict with underwater serpent/panther creatures (similar to the Perun-Veles conflict in Slavic mythology). A protector spirit.

**The Great Spirit / Creator:**
- **Wakan Tanka** (Lakota) — "the Great Mystery"; the sacred force pervading all things
- **Gitche Manitou** (Anishinaabe) — the Great Spirit; supreme creator
- Distinct from the Abrahamic God; often a more diffuse, immanent concept

### Creation Stories (Selected)

**Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) — Sky Woman:**
A pregnant woman, Sky Woman (Ataensic/Aataentsic), fell from the sky world through a hole created when the Great Tree was uprooted. Water animals tried to help her. Turtle offered its back (hence "Turtle Island" = North America). Muskrat dove to the bottom and brought up mud, which was placed on Turtle's back and grew into the earth. Sky Woman's twin grandsons created aspects of the world: one (Sapling/Teharonhiawako) created good things; the other (Flint/Tawiskaron) created hardship and challenges.

**Navajo (Dine) — Emergence Myth:**
The people emerged through several underworlds (colored worlds — black, blue, yellow, white/glittering) before reaching the present Fifth World. First Man and First Woman, Coyote, and other beings participated. **Changing Woman** (Asdzaa Nadleehe) is the most important deity — associated with the seasons and the earth's renewal; mother of the Hero Twins (Monster Slayer and Born for Water), who rid the world of monsters.

**Lakota — White Buffalo Calf Woman:**
A sacred woman appeared to two Lakota scouts, bringing the sacred pipe (chanunpa) and the Seven Sacred Rites. She departed, transforming into a white buffalo calf. The White Buffalo is sacred.

**Ojibwe — Nanabozho and the Flood:**
After a great flood, Nanabozho (on a log with animals) asked various animals to dive for earth. Muskrat succeeded but died from the effort. Nanabozho used the mud to recreate the earth (Earth Diver motif — very widespread).

### Spirit World Concepts

- The natural world is alive with spirits; animals, plants, rocks, water — all have spiritual power
- **Vision quests** — solitary fasting and prayer to receive spiritual guidance
- **Medicine wheels**, sacred directions (four cardinal directions associated with colors, animals, seasons), and sacred mountains/landscapes
- **Skinwalkers** (Navajo — yee naaldlooshii) — malevolent witches who can transform into animals
- **Wendigo** (Algonquian) — monstrous cannibalistic spirit associated with winter, starvation, and greed; those who resort to cannibalism become Wendigos

## 14. KOREAN MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

**Samguk Yusa** (Memorabilia of the Three Kingdoms, 13th century), **Samguk Sagi** (History of the Three Kingdoms), shamanic songs and rituals (muga/gut), folk tales.

### The Dangun Creation Myth

**Hwanin** (the Lord of Heaven) had a son, **Hwanung**, who wished to live among humans. Hwanin sent him to Mount Taebaek with 3,000 followers and the three celestial seals.

Hwanung established the "City of God" (Sinsi) and ruled over wind, rain, and clouds. He taught humans agriculture, medicine, law, and morality.

A **bear** and a **tiger** came to Hwanung, wishing to become human. He gave them twenty cloves of garlic and a bundle of mugwort, telling them to eat only this and stay in a cave for 100 days. The tiger gave up and left. The bear persevered, transforming into a **woman** (Ungnyeo).

Ungnyeo prayed for a child. Hwanung married her, and she bore **Dangun Wanggeom**, who founded the kingdom of **Gojoseon** (Ancient Joseon) in 2333 BCE (traditional date), establishing the Korean nation. Dangun is considered the legendary progenitor of the Korean people.

| Figure | Domain | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Hwanin** | Heaven, supreme deity | Ruler of heaven |
| **Hwanung** | Civilization | Son of Hwanin; brought civilization to earth |
| **Dangun** | Founding ancestor | Founded Gojoseon |
| **Mago** | Creation goddess | In some traditions, a primordial goddess who created the world; associated with the earth and mountains |
| **Jeoseung Saja** | Death messenger | Grim reaper figure; escorts souls to the afterlife |
| **Sanshin** | Mountain spirits | Mountain gods; very important in Korean folk religion; often depicted as old men with tigers |
| **Yongwang** | Dragon King of the Sea | Rules the underwater palace; can grant wishes; dragons are generally benevolent in Korean mythology |
| **Haemosu** | Sun god | Founder of Buyeo kingdom; father of Jumong |
| **Jumong** | Founder of Goguryeo | Legendary archer and king; miraculous birth (born from an egg after his mother was impregnated by sunlight) |

| Creature | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Dokkaebi** | Korean goblins/trolls; formed from discarded objects imbued with human spirit; mischievous but not evil; love wrestling and games; carry a magical club (dokkaebi bangmangi) that can summon objects; have one leg in some traditions; can be outwitted |
| **Gumiho** | Nine-tailed fox; similar to Chinese huli jing and Japanese kitsune; usually a malevolent female who eats human hearts or livers to become fully human; some stories feature sympathetic gumiho |

### Key Legends

**Princess Bari:** A princess abandoned at birth (seventh daughter when a son was desired). She journeys to the underworld to obtain the elixir of life to save her dying parents. She endures many hardships, ultimately succeeding. She becomes the first mudang (shaman) and the goddess who guides the dead. This myth is central to Korean shamanism (gut rituals).

**The Woodcutter and the Heavenly Maiden:** A woodcutter hides a celestial maiden's clothes while she bathes, forcing her to stay. They marry and have children. Eventually she finds her clothes and returns to heaven with the children. He follows via a magical bucket drawn by a rooster. They are reunited as constellations.

**Simcheong:** A filial daughter who sacrifices herself (throws herself into the sea) so her blind father can regain his sight. She is reborn from a lotus flower and becomes queen.

## 15. PERSIAN/ZOROASTRIAN MYTHOLOGY

### Sources

**Avesta** (Zoroastrian scripture, especially the Gathas attributed to Zarathustra/Zoroaster), **Shahnameh** (Book of Kings, by Ferdowsi, c. 1010 CE — the Persian national epic), **Bundahishn** (Zoroastrian creation text), **Denkard**.

### Zoroastrian Cosmology — Cosmic Dualism

The central feature: a cosmic struggle between good and evil.

**Ahura Mazda** (Ohrmazd) — the supreme, uncreated god of wisdom, truth, and light. Creator of all good things. Represents **Asha** (truth, cosmic order).

**Angra Mainyu** (Ahriman) — the destructive spirit; source of all evil, lies, and suffering. Represents **Druj** (falsehood, chaos). Not a god equal to Ahura Mazda but a destructive force that will ultimately be defeated.

This dualism profoundly influenced later religions (Judaism during the Babylonian exile, Christianity, Manichaeism, and Islam). Concepts such as heaven/hell, final judgment, angels/demons, a savior figure, and an eschatological battle all have Zoroastrian antecedents or parallels.

### Creation

Ahura Mazda created the world in seven stages:
1. **Sky** — stone (hard shell protecting creation)
2. **Water**
3. **Earth**
4. **Plants**
5. **Animals** — the Primordial Bull (Gavaevodata)
6. **Humans** — **Gayomart** (first man); from his seed grew Mashya and Mashyana (first human couple)
7. **Fire** — pervading all creation

Angra Mainyu attacked creation, introducing death, disease, pollution, and evil creatures (noxious animals, harsh climate). The history of the world is the ongoing struggle between these forces.

### The Amesha Spentas (Holy Immortals)

Seven divine beings created by Ahura Mazda:

| Name | Domain |
|------|--------|
| **Vohu Manah** | Good Mind |
| **Asha Vahishta** | Best Truth/Righteousness |
| **Khshathra Vairya** | Desirable Dominion |
| **Spenta Armaiti** | Holy Devotion |
| **Haurvatat** | Wholeness/Health |
| **Ameretat** | Immortality |
| **Spenta Mainyu** | Holy Spirit (sometimes identified with Ahura Mazda himself) |

### Other Key Figures

- **Yazatas** — "beings worthy of worship"; divine helpers including Mithra (contracts, justice, sun — later the Roman Mithras), Anahita (water, fertility, warfare — parallels to Ishtar), and Verethragna (victory)
- **Daevas** — demons; evil spirits serving Angra Mainyu (the word "devil" is etymologically related)
- **Fravashis** — guardian spirits of individuals; exist before birth and after death

### Eschatology (Frashokereti — "Making Wonderful")

The world will end in a final renovation:
- A savior figure, the **Saoshyant** (born of Zarathustra's seed preserved in a lake, impregnating a virgin who bathes there), will lead the final battle against evil
- The dead will be resurrected
- A river of molten metal will purify all souls (brief pain for the wicked, warm milk for the righteous)
- Angra Mainyu will be destroyed forever
- The world will be perfected and made eternal — no more death, suffering, or evil

### The Shahnameh — Heroes and Kings

Ferdowsi's Shahnameh is one of the longest epic poems ever written (~50,000 couplets), covering the mythical and historical kings of Iran from creation to the Arab conquest.

**Key Heroes:**

**Rostam** — the greatest hero of the Shahnameh; born of a Caesarean birth (his mother cut open); possessed superhuman strength; rode the magnificent horse **Rakhsh**. His **Seven Labors** (Haft Khan) parallel Heracles' labors:
1. Rakhsh fights a lion
2. Surviving a desert of thirst
3. Slaying a dragon
4. Killing a sorceress
5. Capturing the Demon King
6. Fighting Arzhang Div
7. Defeating the White Demon (Div-e Sepid)

**Tragic story of Rostam and Sohrab:** Rostam unknowingly fights and kills his own son Sohrab in battle (one of literature's most famous tragic stories; translated by Matthew Arnold).

**Siavash:** A prince falsely accused by his stepmother (Sudabeh) — a story paralleling Joseph and Potiphar's wife, and Hippolytus/Phaedra. Siavash proved his innocence by riding through fire. Later murdered, his death sparking a cycle of revenge.

**Zahhak (Azi Dahaka):** A tyrannical king kissed on the shoulders by Angra Mainyu, causing two serpents to grow from his shoulders that required human brains to feed. Ruled for 1,000 years of terror. Overthrown by **Fereydun** (aided by the blacksmith Kaveh, whose leather apron became the royal standard of Iran).

**Key Dynasty/Period Divisions:**
- **Pishdadian** dynasty (mythical first kings: Kayumars/Gayomart, Hushang, Tahmuras, Jamshid)
- **Kayanid** dynasty (Kay Qobad, Kay Kavus, Kay Khosrow)
- Historical dynasties through the Sasanids

This concludes the comprehensive research report. Key notes for building the database:

1. **Interconnections matter:** Many mythological figures appear across traditions or have clear parallels (flood myths, tricksters, world trees, dying gods). A good database should enable cross-referencing.

2. **Oral traditions:** Many traditions (African, Native American, Polynesian, Slavic) rely on oral sources. Written records may reflect the biases of later compilers (often Christian monks or colonial-era scholars). This should be noted in the database for accuracy and cultural sensitivity.

3. **Living traditions:** Hindu, Shinto, Yoruba, and many Native American traditions are living religions, not merely "mythology." The database should approach these with respect and note their ongoing religious significance.

4. **Regional variation:** Within each tradition (especially African, Native American, Polynesian, and Chinese), there are significant regional and temporal variations. The database should note which specific sub-tradition a story comes from.

5. **Source attribution:** Each entry should cite primary sources (Iliad, Kojiki, Popol Vuh, etc.) to maintain scholarly credibility.
